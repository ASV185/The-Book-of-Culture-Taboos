Japanese Cultural Taboos & Practices

Removing Shoes in Japan

Japan has several customs and taboos, such as removing shoes before entering various places. Places that are common to remove shoes are tatami and Japanese homes. Tatami rooms have matting called tatami. These rooms can be expected in ryokans (Japanese-style inns), temples, and traditional restaurants.

Passing Money When Paying in Japan

Money in Japan is not passed around from hand to hand. Many places have a small tray were you place the money or credit cards, and if change is given, it would be place in the same small tray. It is prevalent in various places such as taxis, convenience stores, restaurants, hotels and other places to use this practice.

Standing on the Wrong Side of the Escalator

In most cities in Japan, people stand on the left side of the escalator to leave the right side for people who have to pass quickly. The only city that is the exception to the rule is Osaka, where the practice is reversed.

Chopsticks(Ohashi) Basics

    These are some Chopsticks(ohashi) etiquette:
  • Never point your chopsticks to another person or wave them in the air.
  • Don't stick your chopsticks into a bowl of rice.
  • Don't pass food from chopsticks to chopsticks.
  • When serving yourself a shared dish, use the opposite end of your chopsticks to serve yourself.

Giving Out Tips in Restaurants in Japan

It may be prevalent to give out tips in other countries, but that is not the case in Japan. You do not need to give out tips in places such as restaurants, bars, or taxis. It causes confusion in most scenarios except after an excellent tour. It is acceptable to give a tip for outstanding service in a tour.

Saying 'No' in a Polite Way

In Japan, a direct 'no' can come across as too blunt or impolite. Locals tend to use softer expressions to decline or refuse something instead of saying 'no', but the softer expressions should be taken as a 'no'. If this is not understood, it can cause awkward or uncomfortable situations.
